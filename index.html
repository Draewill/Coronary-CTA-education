Cardiac CTA Scanning

A Comprehensive Guide for Technologists

CTWhat is Cardiac CTA?

Cardiac Computed Tomography Angiography (CTA) is a non-invasive imaging technique that uses X-rays and contrast media to visualize the coronary arteries and cardiac structures with exceptional detail.

Primary Applications:

  • Evaluation of coronary artery disease (CAD)
  • Assessment of coronary artery anatomy before surgical procedures
  • Detection of coronary anomalies
  • Evaluation of bypass grafts and stents
  • Calcium scoring for risk stratification

Cardiac CTA provides superior spatial resolution compared to other non-invasive cardiac imaging modalities and can visualize both coronary arteries and cardiac chambers in a single examination.

1Patient Preparation

Pre-Scan Requirements

  • Heart Rate Control: Target HR <65 bpm (ideally <60 bpm) for optimal image quality
  • Beta-blockers: Administer if HR >65 bpm (metoprolol 25-100mg PO or 5-15mg IV)
  • Sublingual nitroglycerin: 0.4-0.8mg given 3-5 minutes before scan (contraindicated with recent PDE-5 inhibitor use)
  • Fasting: 4 hours prior to scan

Patient Positioning

  • Supine position, arms raised above head
  • ECG leads placement: avoid metal artifacts in scan field
  • Breathing instructions practice before scanning
  • IV line: 18-20 gauge in antecubital vein (right preferred)

3Timing Bolus or Bolus Tracking:

  • Bolus tracking: ROI in descending aorta at carina level (threshold: 100-150 HU)
  • Scan delay: Typically 5-8 seconds after threshold reached

4Contrast Injection Protocol:

  • Contrast volume: 60-100 mL (based on scan time and flow rate)
  • Iodine concentration: 350-400 mg/mL
  • Flow rate: 5-6 mL/sec
  • Saline chaser: 40-50 mL at same flow rate

ECG Gating Strategies

Prospective Triggering

  • When: Regular rhythm, HR <65 bpm
  • Advantage: 70-90% dose reduction
  • Phase: Usually 70-80% of R-R
  • Limitation: Single cardiac phase

Retrospective Gating

  • When: Irregular rhythm, functional analysis needed
  • Advantage: Multiple cardiac phases
  • Phase: Reconstruction at any % of R-R
  • Limitation: Higher radiation dose

Common Artifacts & Solutions

  • Motion artifact: Improve heart rate control, use faster gantry rotation, optimize reconstruction phase
  • Stair-step artifact: Ensure consistent breathing, consider breath-hold coaching
  • Beam hardening: Reduce from metallic objects (pacemaker, ICD), use iterative reconstruction
  • Blooming artifact: From calcium/stents - use sharper kernels, lower kVp in obese patients
  • Contrast streaming: Optimize injection protocol, ensure adequate saline flush

5Post-Processing & Reconstruction

Standard Reconstructions

  • Axial images: 0.5-0.75 mm slices (primary dataset)
  • MPR (Multiplanar Reformation): Coronal and sagittal views
  • MIP (Maximum Intensity Projection): Thin-slab MIP for vessel visualization
  • CPR (Curved Planar Reformation): Following individual coronary arteries
  • 3D Volume Rendering: Global cardiac and coronary anatomy

Phase Selection

For retrospective ECG-gated studies, reconstruct images at multiple phases:

  • Mid-diastole: 70-80% of R-R interval (primary phase for evaluation)
  • End-systole: 40-45% of R-R interval (backup phase)
  • Multiple phases: Every 10% for functional analysis if needed

Radiation Dose ConsiderationsImportant

Dose Reduction Strategies:

  • Use prospective ECG triggering when possible (vs. retrospective)
  • Apply tube current modulation (TCM)
  • Reduce kVp in lower BMI patients (100 kVp instead of 120 kVp)
  • Optimize scan range (limit to cardiac region)
  • Use iterative reconstruction algorithms
  • Target effective dose: 1-5 mSv (prospective), 5-15 mSv (retrospective)
